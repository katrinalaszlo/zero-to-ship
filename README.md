# Zero to Ship

An open-source toolkit for product people who are ready to build.

---

## What this is

Four years ago, I couldn't write code. My background is design thinking and venture, the kind of work where you look at insights across different fields and figure out how to apply them. Not engineering. I worked at an innovation lab where we created MVPs and designs, a lot of which never shipped. Great ideas, sitting in decks.

Then I joined a data company and got comfortable being technical. Not writing code, but understanding systems, reading documentation, talking to engineers in their language. When AI coding tools showed up, something clicked. I went from writing product specs to shipping actual software. I built a startup. I read neural network code and understood it. Not because I became an engineer, but because the tools caught up to the way I already think.

If you're a PM, you know the feeling: you ship something, real users touch it, feedback starts coming in. That's the best part of the job. But too many product people are blocked from ever getting there. They have the instinct, the taste, the vision, but no team to execute. Ideas stay in docs and decks.

That doesn't have to be true anymore. **You can build real software right now, today, with the skills you already have.** This project exists to unblock you.

## The moment we're in

This isn't just a feeling. The shift is happening everywhere:

> "There's a new kind of coding I call 'vibe coding', where you fully give in to the vibes, embrace exponentials, and forget that the code even exists." -- **Andrej Karpathy**, who later evolved this into "agentic engineering": you're not writing code, you're orchestrating agents who do.

But let's be clear: this isn't vibes. It's the structure and systems we already know how to orchestrate. Writing specs, defining scope, running processes, unblocking work. That's not vibing. That's product management. Strategy, orchestration, and design: the things we're best at. And ironically, that's what makes you a really good "vibe coder." The people who are best at this aren't vibing at all. They're the ones who've been running structured processes their whole career.

Karpathy landed on the right destination when he called it "agentic engineering," because that's what it actually is: orchestration with intent.

> "We don't care about professional coders anymore." -- **Amjad Masad, Replit CEO**. 75% of Replit users never write a single line of code.

> "The programming language is human." -- **Jensen Huang, NVIDIA CEO**

25% of Y Combinator's Winter 2025 batch had codebases that were 95% AI-generated. Teams of under 10 people are hitting $1M-$10M in revenue. A PM named Dmitry built and shipped a feature in a single day that would have died in prioritization meetings under normal circumstances. As one article put it: "Shipping became cheaper than explaining."

The skeptics raise fair points: AI handles 80% of a build fluently, but the last 20% (security, edge cases, scale) still requires real understanding. That's exactly why this repo exists. Not to skip the learning, but to give you a path through it.

## Who this is for

- Product managers who've been scoping features for years but never shipped code
- Designers who know exactly what something should look like but rely on engineers to build it
- Anyone who thinks they're "not technical"

Here's the thing: the definition of technical has changed. You used to get stuck because you forgot a comma somewhere and couldn't figure out why everything broke. Now you debug with natural language. The barrier isn't syntax anymore.

What matters now is having vision, managing teams, orchestrating. Assigning work, sending people off to execute, making sure everyone is as efficient as possible, knowing how to unblock or putting someone in place who will. That's product management. That's design thinking. Those skills are critical now in a way they weren't before, because the team you're orchestrating can be AI.

This isn't about replacing engineers. Engineers are needed, and their roles are only going to get more complex upstream. If anything, everyone is converging: engineers are going to have to learn to be a little more like product managers, and we're going to have to learn to be a little more like engineers. This is about closing that gap from our side.

If you can write a clear spec or a detailed bug report, you already have the core skill.

## The idea

Product people already know how to:

- Define what "done" looks like
- Break big problems into small ones
- Write clear instructions for other people (or agents) to execute
- Measure results and iterate

That's 80% of working with AI coding tools. The other 20% is learnable, and this repo teaches it.

## Contribute your journey

This isn't a course. It's a collection of paths.

If you're a PM, designer, or non-technical builder who shipped something with AI tools, add your story. What worked. What didn't. What you wish someone had told you.

1. Fork this repo
2. Add your lesson, blog post, or guide to the relevant folder
3. Open a PR with your story

No gatekeeping. No prerequisites. If you built something and learned something, it belongs here.

## Getting started

If you're brand new:

1. Install [Claude Code](https://docs.anthropic.com/en/docs/claude-code) (or any AI coding tool you prefer)
2. Read the [Learning Map](lessons/learning-map.html) to see the full picture
3. Start with whatever lesson matches where you are right now
4. Build something. Anything. A landing page, a tool, an API. Ship it.

The goal isn't to become an engineer. The goal is to stop being blocked by not having one.
